# Research and Validation

Use this reference when a design decision depends on uncertain user behavior or when the user asks for personas, interviews, journey maps, surveys, analytics interpretation, or usability testing.

## Evidence ladder

Record consequential claims with one of these evidence types:

1. **Observed:** analytics, support data, session behavior, completed research, or direct task observation.
2. **Stated:** a user or customer statement with known source and context.
3. **Domain:** stakeholder or subject-matter knowledge with named assumptions.
4. **Secondary:** market, competitor, or published evidence that may not represent this product's users.
5. **Hypothesis:** a design assumption requiring validation.

For each claim record:

- claim
- evidence type and source
- confidence: high, medium, or low
- decision affected
- validation status
- next validation action when needed

Do not manufacture participant quotes, metrics, pain points, or findings.

## Persona integrity

Use a **validated persona** only when it is grounded in actual research or behavioral evidence. Otherwise create a **proto-persona** and include:

- assumed context and job
- hypothesized behaviors and constraints
- evidence already available
- risky assumptions
- questions that would disprove the persona
- decisions the proto-persona is allowed to influence

Do not add decorative biography details that do not change design decisions.

## Method selection

Choose the lightest method that can change the decision:

- exploratory interview: unknown needs, language, mental models
- contextual observation: real workflow, environment, interruptions, tools
- usability test: whether a proposed flow can be completed
- survey: prevalence after the concepts and answer options are understood
- analytics/support review: observed behavior, abandonment, recurring friction
- competitive review: conventions and gaps, never proof of user need

State why the method fits the decision and what it cannot prove.

## Critical-journey map

For consequential flows, map:

- happy path
- failure path
- difficult, interrupted, low-connectivity, accessibility, or assisted path when relevant

For each stage record user goal, trigger, action, system response, uncertainty, error/recovery, evidence, metric, and team or integration dependency.

## Usability-test plan

A focused plan contains:

- decision being tested
- participant profile and exclusion criteria
- realistic scenario
- three to seven critical tasks
- observable success criteria
- completion, time, error, recovery, and self-reported ease measures
- neutral moderator prompts
- debrief questions
- note-taking structure
- severity and confidence rules

Do not ask leading questions or teach the interface before the task. Separate observed behavior from participant interpretation.
